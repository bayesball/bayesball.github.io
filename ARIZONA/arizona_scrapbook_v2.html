Day One · January 19

An Oasis in the Desert

Our first stop was Sweetwater Wetlands — a constructed riparian habitat tucked beside the Santa Cruz River on the edge of Tucson. What looks like it shouldn't exist here absolutely does: glassy ponds edged with cattails, towering cottonwoods blazing yellow, and ducks cruising calmly in all directions. Binoculars around the neck, camera in hand — the afternoon light was perfect.

Wetlands

Sweetwater Wetlands, Tucson

A constructed wetland system that has become one of Tucson's premier birding destinations. The ponds are fed by reclaimed water and support a remarkable variety of waterbirds year-round. On a January afternoon the cattails shimmer and the Santa Catalina Mountains peek above the tree line in the distance.

Ready for Whatever Flies By

Binoculars, camera, a hat against the January sun — fully equipped for a serious afternoon of birding. The mountain range rising behind the pond is the Santa Catalinas, which would become a recurring backdrop throughout the trip.

Day One · Evening

The Mountains on Fire

As the sun dropped, the Santa Catalinas lit up in shades of copper and amber — the phenomenon locals call "alpenglow." Even from a street corner in town, the spectacle stopped us cold.

Landscape

Alpenglow on the Santa Catalinas

The Santa Catalina Mountains reach nearly 9,200 feet at their highest point (Mount Lemmon), and when the setting sun catches the upper ridgelines at low angle, the entire range seems to glow from within. This January evening painted them a deep terracotta — an unforgettable first-night welcome to the Sonoran Desert.

Day Two · January 20

Madera Canyon — Where the Birds Are

Madera Canyon in the Santa Rita Mountains is one of the most celebrated birding destinations in North America, drawing visitors from around the world who come hoping to spot elegant trogons, rare owls, and a staggering variety of hummingbirds. The canyon sits at around 4,000–5,000 feet elevation — high enough for oaks and junipers, low enough for Sonoran species. We arrived at dawn and stayed until noon.

Mountains

First Light on the Santa Ritas

The rocky summit of Mount Wrightson (9,453 ft) catches the first rays of morning. Below it, the canyon's oak-juniper woodland spreads across the slopes in every shade of grey-green. The Sonoran Desert transitions abruptly to sky-island forest here — one of the ecological wonders of southern Arizona.

Trail

The Canyon Trail

A paved interpretive path winds through alligator juniper and Emory oak, dappled with morning shadow. Arizona's sky islands — isolated mountain ranges surrounded by desert — harbor species found nowhere else in the United States, making this woodland feel more like a Mexican highland than the American Southwest.

Wildlife

An Unhurried Encounter

A white-tailed deer grazes calmly on the path, completely unbothered by our presence. Coues white-tailed deer — a small subspecies native to the sky islands of southern Arizona and northern Mexico — are common in Madera Canyon, often seen browsing in the early morning along the lower trails.

Birdlife

The Wildlife Area

Madera Canyon's famous feeding station draws an extraordinary cast of characters. Here a pair of Montezuma quail (also known as harlequin quail) scratch beneath the seed feeders — a bird so cryptically patterned it can vanish in plain sight. The station's multiple feeders attract dozens of species daily, making it one of the easiest places in North America to observe Mexican border specialties.

Birdlife

A Flash of Iridescence

A hummingbird — most likely a broad-billed hummingbird given the turquoise-green sheen — hovers beside a feeder in the oak woodland. Southern Arizona in winter is one of the few places in the US where multiple hummingbird species remain year-round, drawn by an abundance of feeders and the mild canyon climate.

"The sky islands of southern Arizona are among the most biodiverse places in North America — where Sonoran desert meets Mexican highland forest, and the birds of two countries overlap."
— A note from the trail

Day Three · January 21

Sabino Canyon — Into the Catalinas

Just northeast of Tucson, Sabino Canyon cuts dramatically into the Santa Catalina Mountains. A seasonal stream carves through layered granite and limestone, creating rock pools, dry waterfalls, and corridors of saguaro that climb impossibly steep slopes. We hiked the canyon bottom trail and scrambled to a high viewpoint — one of the best mornings of the whole trip.

Landscape

A Thousand Saguaros

The saguaro (Carnegiea gigantea) is the icon of the Sonoran Desert and reaches its densest stands on the rocky slopes of the Santa Catalinas. A mature saguaro can live 150–200 years, weigh several tons when fully hydrated, and support an entire community of birds, bats, and insects. The hillsides of Sabino Canyon are packed with them — their upward-reaching arms giving the landscape an almost human quality.

Canyon

Looking Up from the Creek Bed

The canyon walls rise sharply above the boulder-strewn creek bed. Saguaros find improbable purchase on the cliff faces, their roots gripping the fractured rock. In flash flood season, the entire canyon bottom becomes a rushing river — for now, a dry and peaceful scramble.

Geology

Rock and Cactus

Layered Precambrian schist forms dramatic cliff faces, tilted and fractured by millions of years of tectonic activity. The saguaros colonize every ledge and crack, their roots anchored in the thinnest soils. The contrast of warm rust-colored rock against the cool blue-green of the cacti is quintessentially Sonoran.

Trail

Walking the Wash

The trail follows the dry creek bed — a mix of smooth gravel, round boulders, and sandy stretches. Desert bunch grasses line the banks, and around each bend a new configuration of rock and cactus opens up. This is a hike that rewards patience: the canyon reveals itself slowly.

Water

A Hidden Pool

Sabino Creek forms tinajas — natural rock pools — where water persists even in dry winter months. These pools are vital wildlife watering holes and create surprisingly lush micro-habitats in an otherwise arid landscape. Standing beside one, with saguaros climbing the slopes above and golden cottonwoods along the banks, is genuinely magical.

Color

Gold in the Canyon

Fremont cottonwoods turn brilliant gold along the canyon stream in late autumn and early winter — a surprising burst of warm color among the grey-green desert palette. The juxtaposition of the autumn cottonwoods with towering saguaros is one of those only-in-Arizona scenes that stops you mid-stride.

Hiking

The Ledge Trail

A narrow path carved along the canyon wall requires some careful footwork — and rewards it with views straight down to the creek and across to the opposite cliff face, where saguaros cling to every available surface. The golden cottonwood at left marks the stream corridor below. This is Sabino Canyon at its most dramatic.

Day Three · Afternoon

Agua Caliente Park

After the canyon, we drove east to Agua Caliente Regional Park — a spring-fed oasis at the foot of the Rincon Mountains. The warm spring water feeds a pair of ponds ringed by Washington fan palms, creating a scene that feels more like a California mission garden than a Tucson park.

Park

The Spring-Fed Pond at Agua Caliente

Washington fan palms (Washingtonia filifera) — the only palm native to western North America — line the banks of the warm-spring pond at Agua Caliente Park. The spring has supported human habitation for at least 4,000 years; the site was a Hohokam settlement, then a Spanish colonial ranch, and now a beloved Pima County park. The Rincon Mountains rise behind the palms, completing a scene of layered Arizona history.

Day Four · January 22

Reid Park — City Birding

Not every great birding spot is wilderness. Reid Park, tucked into a Tucson neighborhood, hosts a lake that attracts impressive flocks of diving ducks through the winter months. A perfect morning stroll between adventures.

Birdlife

A Raft of Ring-Necked Ducks

Dozens of ring-necked ducks pack the blue waters of Reid Park lake, along with a few mallards cruising the margins. Ring-necked ducks are winter visitors to southern Arizona, arriving from their northern breeding grounds each October and lingering through March. Despite the name, the "ring" on the neck is nearly invisible in the field — birders joke they should have been named "ring-billed ducks" for the much more visible white ring on the bill.

Garden

Saguaros & Brittlebush in Bloom

Yellow brittlebush (Encelia farinosa) bursts into early bloom along a garden path framed by towering saguaros. Brittlebush is one of the Sonoran Desert's most cheerful plants — after winter rains it carpets entire hillsides in yellow. The silvery-grey foliage is an adaptation to reflect intense desert sunlight. The path's decorative wall, painted with roadrunners, is a quintessentially Tucson touch.

Days Five & Six · January 23–24

Game, Set, Arizona

The trip wasn't only about birds and canyons — we were also here for the USTA National Championships. Tucson's dry winter climate and abundant outdoor courts make it a premier destination for competitive tennis, and watching high-level play under the January sun was the perfect capstone to the week.

Tennis

USTA National Championships

The United States Tennis Association's National Championships bring competitive players from across the country to Tucson each January. The tournament spans multiple age divisions and skill levels, filling the courts with high-quality tennis in ideal outdoor conditions — warm sun, little wind, and the Santa Catalinas visible beyond the fences.

Tennis

Full Extension

A player launches into a serve, fully airborne at the moment of contact — the kind of athletic commitment that separates competitive from recreational tennis. The hard blue-and-green surface, the chain-link frame, the white hat in the bright afternoon light: a classic outdoor tennis scene, elevated by the setting. It was thrilling to watch.
